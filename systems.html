The Systems

Neurotransmitters do not encode specific thoughts. Instead, they modulate the probabilities and patterns of neuronal firing. They set the stage upon which neural circuits perform, influencing what mental states are possible or likely. Here we explore the primary systems in detail.

Glutamate

Excitatory Overdrive and Circuit Rigidity

Glutamate is the brain's primary excitatory neurotransmitter. It is crucial for synaptic plasticity and neural network activation. Dysregulation of glutamate has been increasingly linked to repetitive and rigid cognitive patterns. In conditions of excitatory overdrive, certain brain circuits can become "locked" into persistent activity. This state correlates with obsessive thoughts or excessive focus on negative themes.

Magnetic resonance spectroscopy (MRS) studies show that OCD patients have elevated glutamate levels in key frontal regions, such as the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) and striatum. This chemical imbalance likely makes the ACC hyperactive and unable to "shift gears," contributing to intrusive thoughts and compulsive urges that the patient cannot easily suppress.[5][6]

Synaptic Atrophy and Stress

While acute glutamate activity is necessary for learning, chronic stress can lead to excitotoxicity. This physical loss of connectivity, particularly in the prefrontal cortex, correlates with the persistence of depressive symptoms and cognitive rigidity.

GABA

Inhibition and Thought Suppression

Gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) is the brain's primary inhibitory neurotransmitter. It opposes glutamate, acting as the system's brake. Insufficient GABAergic activity leads to a loss of neural inhibition, allowing intrusive thoughts and anxieties to run unchecked.

Low cortical GABA levels have been consistently observed in depression. GABAergic interneurons in the frontal cortex normally help filter out repetitive negative thoughts. When GABA tone is low, the "signal-to-noise" ratio for negative cognitions worsens.

Serotonin

Mood and Repetitive Thinking

Serotonin (5-HT) is a key modulator implicated in mood regulation and cognitive flexibility. Low serotonergic activity is linked to a bias toward negative information processing, which can fuel rumination and pessimistic outlooks. On a systems level, serotonin helps regulate the brain's Default Mode Network (DMN), which is active during self-referential thought.

Depression is associated with hyperconnectivity of the DMN, which correlates with rumination. Serotonin acts as a mood stabilizer, broadly dampening the intensity of negative self-referential thinking and promoting mental flexibility. Pharmacologically, enhancing 5-HT (with SSRIs) remains one of the most effective strategies for breaking cycles of rumination.[2]

Dopamine

Salience, Reward, and Habit Loops

Dopamine (DA) is associated with reward processing, motivation, and salience attribution. Dopamine imbalances can contribute to harmful cognitive loops by over-assigning significance or driving compulsive behavior. In obsessive-compulsive loops, for example, there is evidence of elevated dopamine activity in the basal ganglia of OCD patients.

This implies that too much dopamine "drive" in the wrong circuits can lock the brain into repetitive loops of thought and behavior. On the other hand, some aspects of rumination in depression may involve low dopamine function, leading to apathy and a lack of rewarding experiences.[1]

Norepinephrine

Arousal, Alarm, and Stress Circuits

Norepinephrine (NE) is a neurotransmitter of arousal and stress response. It amplifies alertness and vigilance. These functions, when exaggerated, can contribute to catastrophic thinking and intrusive memories of threat. Hyperactivity in the NE system is strongly linked to anxiety disorders and PTSD.

Under acute stress, the brain is flooded with norepinephrine, which heightens focus on potential dangers. Chronically elevated NE imprints a state of constant alarm. Catastrophizing (continually anticipating disaster) may stem from an NE system that is unable to return to baseline. Excess norepinephrine makes the brain selectively attentive to negative or fear-provoking stimuli.

Acetylcholine

Cognitive Bias and Tone

Acetylcholine (ACh) is associated with attention, learning, and memory. Recent research suggests the cholinergic system plays a role in mood and repetitive cognition. An old hypothesis posits an imbalance between acetylcholine and monoamines in depression.

A striking example is the rapid antidepressant effects of scopolamine, a muscarinic acetylcholine receptor antagonist. Patients on scopolamine report lifted mood and notably less negative rumination. One explanation is that an overactive cholinergic system causes a bias toward negative and self-critical thinking, heightening focus on internal details.

Other Modulators

Endocannabinoids and Dynorphins

Other systems play important modulatory roles. The Endocannabinoid (eCB) System is sometimes called the "stress recovery" system. It helps the brain return to baseline after stress and facilitates the extinction of fearful memories. Deficits in eCB signaling have been linked to anxiety, depression, and PTSD.

The Dynorphin/Kappa-Opioid System is part of the brain's "anti-reward" machinery. When activated, it produces dysphoria, stress, and motivation loss. During chronic stress, dynorphin is released, leading to intense dysphoric feelings. Blocking this system has shown antidepressant effects in early trials.
